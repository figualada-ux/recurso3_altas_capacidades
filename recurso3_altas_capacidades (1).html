i.

Reading between the lines

Nuance · will vs. going to vs. present continuous

Grammar rules are only a start — real usage depends on what the speaker means. Read each situation carefully, choose the best option and explain why. Then reveal an expert answer and compare.

Situation 01

The phone rings. No one is moving. Your mum sighs and you say to her, "Don't worry, I _____ it."

Choose the best form

Justify your choice (1–2 sentences)

Expert analysis The correct answer is will get. The decision to answer the phone is made at the exact moment of speaking — a spontaneous reaction. Going to would suggest a prior plan (impossible — the phone just rang). Am getting (present continuous) would suggest a fixed, arranged plan, which is also wrong here.
Situation 02

Your cousin has been saving money for a year. Every week she studies maps of Thailand. When a friend asks about her summer, she replies: "I _____ Thailand in August."

Choose the best form

Justify your choice (1–2 sentences)

Expert analysis The correct answer is am going to visit. The context shows intention and preparation over time (saving money, studying maps). That's exactly what going to expresses: a plan that has been thought about, not a spontaneous decision (will) and not a confirmed arrangement with tickets and a fixed date (present continuous).
Situation 03

You are looking at your printed boarding pass. The flight number, seat and time are clearly written: BA 469 — 14:20, Saturday. A friend calls. You say: "Sorry, I can't talk long. I _____ to London on Saturday at 14:20."

Choose the best form

Justify your choice (1–2 sentences)

Expert analysis The correct answer is am flying. The present continuous is used for arrangements that are already confirmed — the ticket exists, the time is fixed, there is no uncertainty. Though am going to fly is grammatically possible, it sounds less natural when the arrangement is so concrete.
Situation 04

Dark clouds are covering the sky. The wind is strong. You look up and say: "It _____."

Choose the best form

Justify your choice (1–2 sentences)

Expert analysis The correct answer is is going to rain. We use going to for predictions based on present evidence we can see (clouds, wind). Will is for predictions based on opinion or belief with no visible evidence ("I think it will rain tomorrow"). Is raining describes something happening now — but it isn't raining yet.
ii.

The editor's eye

Error detection · justification · correction

A student wrote this email about her holiday. It contains five grammar errors related to future tenses. Click on the words you think are wrong. When you think you've found them all, reveal the corrections and explanations.

Hi Emma!

I'm so excited — next month we will going to Greece with my family! We are leave on the 12th of July, and our flight is at 6 am. My brother says he will to visit the Acropolis every single day, but I think he'll get bored. Mum bought me a new camera, so I am going take lots of photos. The weather forecast looks amazing — look at this picture, it will be sunny for sure (you can see the clouds going away!).

Love, Lucía ✨

  • 01
    "will going" → "are going" or "will go" You cannot combine will with -ing. Either use going to (plan) or will + base form.
  • 02
    "are leave" → "are leaving" Present continuous for fixed arrangements requires am/is/are + verb -ing form.
  • 03
    "he will to visit" → "he will visit" After will, we use the base form of the verb with no "to".
  • 04
    "am going take" → "am going to take" The structure going to always needs the preposition to before the main verb.
  • 05
    "it will be sunny" → "it is going to be sunny" When the prediction is based on visible present evidence (clouds going away), going to is more natural than will.
iii.

The invitation

Creative writing · 80–100 words · self-assessment

Time to put everything together. Write an invitation letter using all three future forms naturally. Then evaluate your own writing using the rubric below — being your own editor is a key advanced skill.

Your task

You are organising a special holiday activity next summer. Write an invitation letter to a friend (80–100 words). You must include:

  • At least one sentence with will (a promise or offer)
  • At least one sentence with going to (a plan or intention)
  • At least one sentence with present continuous (a fixed arrangement)
  • A polite invitation expression (e.g., Would you like to…? / How about…?)

Scenario: choose one — a week of surfing camp in Cantabria, a cultural trip to Rome, or a survival weekend in the Pyrenees. Be creative with the details.
